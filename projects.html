Toothpaste Dispenser Design Challenge

The challenge was to design a fixture for a manufacturing line which could dispense toothpaste onto a toothbrush without using hands.
The purpose of this design challenge was to demonstrate skills in fixture design and manufacturing process development.

Requirements:
  1. Able to dispense toothpaste reliably with consistent volume
  2. Support manual or auto load and unload toothbrush process
  3. 22 seconds maximum cycle time
  4. Dispense within +/- 0.10 grams
  5. Toothpaste does not drip on the side
  6. Repeatable

Assumptions:
  1. All toothbrush are the same shape and size
  2. Toothbrush arrive to and leave the fixture on a conveyor
  3. Conveyor alignment is accurate within +/- 5mm longitudinal and +/- 1mm lateral, and is repeatable for 50 toothbrushes
  4. Toothbrush head is flat at the bottom
  5. Fixture is isolated from ground vibration caused by other factory equipment
Overview of fixture
1) Fixture
5 toothbrushes from the conveyor align with the fixture. The fixture table lifts so that the brushes rest on the workpiece holders. Alignment blocks on the workpiece holder are actuated by independent solenoids to align and restrict the brush in the X & Y axis. 
2) Dispense and Inspect
A CCD camera is used to check the alignment of the brush. If the brush is misaligned, the alignment blocks are retracted and closed to correct alignment, any subsequent misalignment triggers a line stop. Toothpaste is dispensed using a Hibar pump and Fisnar Valve and nozzle which were chosen to meet the +/- 0.10 gram dispense specification. While dispensing, the nozzle head is moved along the length of the brush at constant speed to evenly dispense toothpaste. The X&Y rail assembly, actuated by stepper motor and belt, moves the head. Upon completion, a UV light illuminates the toothpaste dispensed on the brush while the CCD camera captures an image to check if any toothpaste has dripped to the sides. Toothpaste contains fluoride which glows under UV light, hence an image captured under it will allow the toothpaste to be filtered easily from the surroundings.
3) Repeat and Transfer
The head moves to the next brush, and the dispense and inspect process is repeated. After all brushes are complete, the alignment blocks are retracted, the fixture table lowers to remove the brushes from the workpiece holder, and the conveyor moves forward.

Quadrotor Drone Battery Swapping Station
Final Year Design Project

Quadrotor drones cannot fly for more than 20 minutes due to their inability to carry heavy batteries with larger capacity. For commercial applications such as surveying, monitoring and security, where longer mission times are required, a human operator must be present to recharge or swap batteries of the drones. To overcome this problem, our solution was to develop a system that could swap depleted batteries of quadrotor drones autonomously with no operator assistance. We designed and built a proof of concept for a landing station that could achieve this using a custom designed battery enclosure, battery latching mechanism, landing pad built with alignment ramps, linear actuators for battery transfer, and a drone clamping mechanism. 
Contributions (within team of 4)
  • Designed the clamping mechanism and contributed to the design of the battery enclosure and latching mechanism in 3D CAD. Iterated through several designs and performed validation
  • Sourced parts and contributed to the fabrication and assembly using machine shop tools and equipment such as lathes and mills
  • Built the electronics hardware which consisted of an Arduino microcontroller, motor drivers, ultrasonic sensor, linear actuator and servo and stepper motors
  • Developed separate calibration and demo programs for the Arduino
The Magic Steps
  1. Drone lands on the platform, clamps engage automatically to secure the drone
  2. Discharged battery is removed from the drone and placed into the battery bay
  3. Fresh battery is removed from the battery bay and inserted into the drone
  4. Clamps disengage, the drone is ready to fly
Design
Clamp mechanism CAD model
Clamp mechanism prototype
A threaded rod mounted to a servo motor, that runs two clamps mounted in a T-formation. An internally-threaded block is mounted to the threaded rod. When the servo runs, the block moves forward. This causes two link arms to laterally push two clamps mounted to linear rails, outwards.
Battery enclosure and battery bay CAD model
Prototype. Battery enclosure with battery being inserted into bay on drone
Grab catch latch used to hold battery with enclosure inside bay
Servo motor actuated hook to grab battery
The battery is encased in a battery enclosure which can slide into and out of a battery bay on the Quadrotor drone. The battery bay consists of side rails with lead in chamfers and a grab catch latch. Both the bay and enclosure were machined on a mill. The grab catch consists of a strike plate which pushes into a spring loaded catch. It is rated at its closure force and was chosen to ensure it can hold the weight of the battery during flight. A hook actuated by a servo motor was attached to the end of a linear actuator which pulled and inserted the battery into the bay.
